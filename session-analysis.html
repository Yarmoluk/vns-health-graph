VNSNY CHOICE Provider Manual & Reference Guide

Source: vnshealthplans.org (public PDF)

Key data: 90-day timely filing. Secondary claims 60 days from EOB. Corrected claims: UB-04 frequency code 7, CMS-1500 resubmission code 7. Dispute timelines: LHCSAs/SNFs 60 days, meals 90 days, ancillary 6 months. Varis post-payment review: 30-day records request, full reclamation on non-response.

Critical detail: Cross-year billing rule explicitly documented: "Services from 2025 and 2026 must be billed on separate claims. Submitting multiple service years on one claim may result in denials or delayed payment." This is generating predictable denials right now in Q1 2026.

CMS Provider Directory Compliance Requirements

Source: CMS regulations, Quest Analytics policy analysis, Ameridial compliance analysis

Key data: Provider info must be verified every 90 days. Directory updates within 30 days of change. Annual attestation required. Non-compliance: directory suppressed on Medicare Plan Finder. CAPs issued not for inaccurate data, but for inability to demonstrate when the plan became aware of the inaccuracy and what it did in response.

This is the single most important finding: It reframes the compliance problem from data accuracy to process documentation. VNS may have perfectly accurate data — and still get a CAP if they can't prove the awareness timeline.

VNS Health — The Challenges

Five specific operational friction points surfaced from the analysis. Each one is connected to the others. Here's the summary with the cascade logic.

Challenge #1: The CMS Awareness Gap — "When Did You Know?"

CMS Corrective Action Plans are issued not because directory data is wrong, but because the plan cannot demonstrate when it became aware of an inaccuracy and what it did in response. VNS can show current state. It cannot prove awareness timeline. This is a process documentation problem masquerading as a data accuracy problem.

Cascade: Provider changes → not captured with source/timestamp → CMS audit → can't prove awareness → CAP → directory suppressed on Medicare Plan Finder → enrollment growth blocked → 25-county expansion at risk.

At stake: Entire geographic expansion strategy.

Challenge #2: Four-Vendor Fragmentation

Providers navigate Availity (claims), eviCore (auth), Varis (audits), and VNS's own portal — four separate systems with four logins, four status vocabularies, four SLAs, and four support channels. No unified view of the claim lifecycle exists for providers or for VNS internally.

Additionally, the cross-year billing rule (2025/2026 services on separate claims) is generating predictable, preventable denials right now.

Cascade: Vendor fragmentation → provider admin burden → filing errors → denials → disputes → provider fatigue → network attrition.

At stake: $300K+/yr in avoidable admin cost. Provider satisfaction and retention.

Challenge #3: One-Size-Fits-All Engagement (Confirmed by VNS Leadership)

15,000 new members (D-SNP at 60% growth, EasyCare at 34%) are receiving engagement designed for legacy MLTC members. A 45-year-old Medicare HMO member choosing a $25/month plan does not want the same outreach as an 80-year-old with a home health aide.

VNS's own leadership confirms this. SVP Mihriban Gursoy Brickner identified a primary challenge as developing "more creative member engagement strategies for newer plan populations with different needs." This isn't our observation — it's their stated gap.

The blended star rating masks this. Segment-specific satisfaction problems won't surface in annual ratings for 12-18 months — by which time churn has already occurred.

Cascade: Same playbook → unmet expectations by segment → satisfaction divergence → CAHPS impact → star rating erosion → enrollment slowdown.

At stake: $7.5M-$11.25M/yr in churn-driven revenue loss.

Challenge #4: The Varis Recoupment → Network Attrition Link

Varis post-payment reviews give providers 30 days to produce records. Non-response = full claim reclamation. The power dynamic is asymmetric: provider did the work, patient got care, but a missed admin deadline = zero pay. After 1-2 recoupments, providers leave the network. Nobody at VNS connects "network attrition" to the Varis trigger.

Cascade: Varis recoupment → provider revenue loss → provider leaves network → member disrupted → CAHPS impact → star rating impact.

At stake: Network adequacy, especially in expansion counties.

Challenge #5: Institutional Knowledge Drain

130 years of operational intelligence lives in people, not systems. Which providers return calls, which agencies are reliable in which neighborhoods, which members need family contacted first. When veteran staff retire — especially multilingual staff — entire relationship networks vanish.

The 50+ language capability, VNS's most defensible moat, is maintained by people. A Mandarin-speaking care manager who retires takes the entire Mandarin-speaking member-provider network in their territory.

Cascade: Retirements → knowledge loss → worse outcomes → staff overload on remaining → more burnout → more departures → accelerating drain.

At stake: Irreversible competitive advantage loss.

Convergence — How Everything Connects

The One Pattern

Every challenge traces back to the same structural gap: VNS Health has the data, but it's not connected. Provider data lives in one system. Claims data lives in vendor systems. Member engagement data lives in another. Quality measures are tracked monthly but not linked to the provider and member data that drives them. Workforce data (language capability, caseloads, tenure) isn't mapped against member needs by geography.

The data exists. The relationships between the data don't. That's the gap. And it's the same gap causing every friction point — from CMS compliance to member churn to provider attrition to knowledge loss.

Key Takeaways

1. VNS Health is a genuinely excellent organization with solvable problems

5-star quality ratings, 60% enrollment growth, 50+ language capability, 130 years of community trust, and a data-driven quality culture. The problems identified are operational friction — not structural failure. Every one of them has a clear path to resolution.

2. The data exists — the connections don't

VNS doesn't have a data collection problem. It has a data connection problem. Provider data, claims data, member engagement data, quality metrics, and workforce data all exist in separate systems. The friction points emerge from the gaps between those systems — and they compound into each other.

3. Provider data provenance is the single highest-leverage fix

The "when did you know?" problem blocks the 25-county expansion, creates CMS compliance risk, and undermines provider directory accuracy — which is the node where growth, compliance, member experience, and star ratings all intersect. Solve this one problem and you unblock everything downstream.

4. The blended star rating is hiding a ticking clock

Overall satisfaction looks strong because legacy MLTC scores are excellent. But new D-SNP and EasyCare populations have fundamentally different needs. The blended metric masks segment-specific deterioration until it shows up in annual ratings — 12-18 months too late. This needs to be measured at the segment level starting now.

5. The 50+ language moat is the most fragile competitive advantage

It's real, it's defensible, and it's irreplaceable — but it's maintained entirely by people. Every multilingual staff retirement is an unrecoverable loss unless the relationships and operational knowledge they carry are captured before they leave. This isn't urgent today. It's urgent before it becomes urgent.

6. This process itself is the proof of concept

In one session, from public data only: 36 entities extracted, 32 relationships mapped, 5 friction points identified with revenue math, 6 value streams defined, and a 12-slide interactive presentation deployed live. Six iterations driven by real-time feedback. The methodology works. The question is what it reveals when applied to actual operational data.
